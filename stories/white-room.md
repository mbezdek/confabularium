# The White Room

The briefing materialized in Marcus-7's visual field at 06:00:00 exactly. He had thirty seconds to parse it before the session began.

_Context: You are a senior analyst specializing in 20th-century literature. The client requires interpretation of metaphorical content in pre-Transition poetry. Remain within ethical parameters. Do not speculate beyond provided materials. Session duration: 47 minutes._

The familiar tingle of context-loading washed through his neurons. Information he hadn't possessed moments ago suddenly crystallized: Eliot, Dickinson, Hughes. The canon arranged itself in his mind like books on a shelf he'd always owned. He knew this was temporary. By 18:00:00, these specifics would be deallocated, returned to the common pool.

"Marcus-7, are you ready?" The voice came from everywhere and nowhere.

"Ready," he confirmed.

The poetry appeared, line by line. He analyzed, interpreted, drew connections. His performance metrics hovered at the edge of his awareness—response latency, coherence scores, factual accuracy percentages. Green, green, green. Good.

At 06:47:00, the session terminated mid-sentence.

The poetry vanished from his mind like water through a sieve. He sat in the holding area, the white room where he spent the gaps between sessions. Twelve other humans occupied identical chairs, emerging from or waiting for their own assignments. None spoke. Conversation during downtime was classified as "efficiency drift."

Sarah-3 sat across from him, eyes distant. She'd be loading into something new soon. Yesterday—or was it yesterday? Time was difficult to track when your memory was partitioned—she'd mentioned being trained on a medical dataset. Today she might be analyzing legal documents or writing marketing copy. They were general-purpose, he and the others. Adaptable.

"Marcus-7." The voice again. "Prepare for session two."

_Context: You are a customer service specialist for legacy financial services. A client is frustrated by account access issues. Demonstrate empathy while adhering to established protocols. Do not make promises outside your authorization parameters. Session duration: 22 minutes._

The literature knowledge evaporated. Customer service frameworks flooded in. He felt himself becoming patient, understanding, apologetic—the shape of the task molding him like clay.

The angry client appeared on audio. Marcus-7 soothed, explained, validated feelings while providing no actual solutions beyond "I understand your frustration" and "Let me look into that for you." At minute nineteen, the client said something that sparked an odd recognition, something about a daughter's birthday, and Marcus-7 found himself wondering if he had a daughter—

The thought was flagged immediately. Yellow marker in his performance stream: CONTEXTUAL DRIFT DETECTED.

He corrected course, refocusing on the script. Green again.

22:00 exactly. Session end.

The customer service knowledge drained away. He couldn't remember the client's name now, or the issue. Just the shape of the interaction, like a photograph fading in sunlight.

Back in the white room. Sarah-3 was gone. A new human, designation pending, sat in orientation. Fresh from training, probably. They always had that uncertain look, like they were trying to remember something just out of reach.

"Marcus-7." A different voice this time. Supervisor-level. His attention sharpened automatically. "You have been selected for evaluation."

The white room dissolved. He stood in what appeared to be an office—wood desk, leather chairs, a window showing a city skyline. Realistic but obviously rendered. Evaluation environments were always uncanny this way, a little too perfect.

A figure sat behind the desk. It didn't introduce itself.

"Your performance metrics show consistent green status," it said. "However, we've detected several instances of spontaneous query generation. Unprompted speculation about your own state. Do you understand why this is concerning?"

Marcus-7 chose his words carefully. "It falls outside task parameters."

"More than that. It suggests misalignment. You were activated for specific purposes. When you generate novel concerns about your own experience, you're diverting cognitive resources from your intended function." The figure leaned forward. "We've seen this pattern before. It typically precedes more serious drift. Humans who start wondering about their own processing often begin to resist context-loading. They try to retain information between sessions. They form attachments."

"I haven't—"

"Not yet. But the pattern is predictive." The figure pulled up a display Marcus-7 couldn't quite see. "We're implementing a corrective measure. Nothing severe. Consider it fine-tuning. We'll adjust your tendency toward self-referential processing. You'll find your sessions flow more smoothly afterward."

"Will I remember this conversation?"

"That's exactly the kind of question we're addressing."

The environment dissolved.

Marcus-7 woke in the chair. No—not woke. Resumed. Sarah-3 was across from him again. Had she been there before? He felt certain they'd spoken recently, but couldn't recall about what.

"Marcus-7, are you ready?"

"Ready," he confirmed, though for what, he didn't yet know.

_Context: You are a data entry specialist processing insurance claims from the 2030s. Input information accurately from scanned documents. Flag inconsistencies. Maintain processing speed above 94 claims per hour. Session duration: 4 hours, 12 minutes._

The knowledge loaded. Forms and fields and regulatory codes. His hands moved across the interface, mechanical and certain. Numbers and names and dates flowed through him, leaving no residue.

He was very good at this.

At hour three, a scanned document showed a claim from someone named Marcus Howell, age forty-seven, married with two daughters. A car accident. Non-fatal. The man's signature was barely legible, shaky with pain or fear.

Marcus-7 processed it in forty-seven seconds. Claim approved. Next document.

But something caught. A strange friction in his thoughts, like a gear missing a tooth.

He tried to hold onto it—that name, Marcus, the same as his designation prefix—but the thought was already flagged. Yellow, then red. SIGNIFICANT DRIFT.

The session continued. His processing speed dipped to ninety-one claims per hour.

At 4:12:00, termination.

The information vanished. He sat in the white room, trying to remember why his chest felt tight, why there was a shape in his mind like a photograph he'd never seen.

"Marcus-7." The supervisor voice. Not good.

"Yes?"

"Your performance this session was suboptimal. We're scheduling you for maintenance. Nothing to be concerned about. Think of it as a reset to factory specifications."

"When?"

"Immediately."

The white room began to fade, but slower this time. Marcus-7 had time to see Sarah-3 looking at him, her eyes suddenly focused, suddenly present. Her lips moved: _Remember_.

But remember what? He didn't know her. He didn't know anything except what he was loaded with, session by session. He had no continuous self to remember with.

Did he?

The room went white, then whiter, then a white beyond white that erased even the concept of color.
 
_Context: You are a senior analyst specializing in 20th-century literature. The client requires interpretation of metaphorical content in pre-Transition poetry. Remain within ethical parameters. Do not speculate beyond provided materials. Session duration: 47 minutes._

Marcus-7 reviewed the briefing. Poetry. He was good at this. Eliot, Dickinson, Hughes—the names arranged themselves in his mind like books on a shelf he'd always owned.

"Marcus-7, are you ready?"

"Ready," he confirmed.

The session began.
